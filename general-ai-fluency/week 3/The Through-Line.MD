## Step 1: One-Line Claim

**Ten options, generated then narrowed:**

1. I turn ambiguous data into decisions people can act on.
2. I build machine learning models that tell content teams where to look first.
3. Practical machine learning, built for real decisions — not just demos.
4. I turn search performance data into a prioritized to-do list for content teams.
5. I make machine learning explainable enough that a content team can trust it.
6. From raw search signals to a ranked list of what to fix first — that's what I build.
7. I build decision-support models, not black boxes.
8. I show my reasoning, not just my results.
9. I turn "we should probably review this page" into a ranked, defensible list.
10. Machine learning that content teams can actually act on, not just admire.

**Picked and sharpened (combining #4 and #9):**

> **I turn scattered search signals into ranked, evidence-based recommendations for which pages to review first.**

**Why this one:** It's concrete (not "I do machine learning," but what the model actually outputs), it's provable by the capstone itself, and it speaks directly to what a hiring manager wants to know — can this person turn messy data into something a team can act on. "Review first" (rather than "fix first") also keeps the claim honest to what the model actually does — decision-support, not an automatic verdict that a page needs changes.
