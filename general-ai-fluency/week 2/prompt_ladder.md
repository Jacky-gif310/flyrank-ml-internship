# The Prompt Ladder
**Track:** General AI Fluency — applied to a real task (growing my worship music YouTube channel)
**Baseline prompt is intentionally lazy — the kind I'd have typed a month ago.**

---

## Baseline (Version 0)

**Prompt:**
> Help me with marketing my YouTube channel.

**Output (representative excerpt):**
> Sure! Here are some general tips to grow your YouTube channel:
> - Post consistently on a regular schedule
> - Use good thumbnails and titles
> - Optimize your video descriptions with keywords (SEO)
> - Engage with your audience in the comments
> - Promote your videos on social media
> - Collaborate with other creators
> - Analyze your YouTube Analytics to see what's working

**Notes:**
- **What changed in the prompt:** Nothing yet — this is the starting point.
- **What improved in the output:** N/A.
- **What still failed:** Completely generic. Could apply to a cooking channel, a gaming channel, or mine. No mention of worship music, my audience, my current size, or what "help" even means (more views? more subscribers? more comments?). It's advice I could've gotten from a 2015 blog post.
- **What I'd try next:** Give it an actual goal instead of a vague ask for "help."

---

## Version 1 — Add a clearer goal

**Prompt:**
> Help me grow my worship music YouTube channel — specifically, help me figure out how to get from 200 to 1,000 subscribers over the next 3 months.

**Output (representative excerpt):**
> Going from 200 to 1,000 subscribers in 3 months (a 5x increase) is ambitious but possible. Here's a framework:
> 1. **Audit what's already working** — which videos brought in your current 200 subs?
> 2. **Increase upload frequency or consistency** — channels that grow fast in this window typically post 2-3x/week.
> 3. **Double down on discovery** — YouTube Shorts can act as a funnel to your main channel.
> 4. **Set weekly subscriber targets** — ~65-70 new subs/week to hit 1,000.
> 5. **Track conversion rate** (views → subs) weekly and adjust.

**Notes:**
- **What changed in the prompt:** Added a specific, numeric goal with a timeframe (200→1,000 in 3 months) instead of the vague "help with marketing."
- **What actually improved in the output:** The advice stopped being generic YouTube-growth filler and became goal-oriented — it did the math on what weekly sub growth I'd actually need, and it framed tactics around hitting a target rather than just "best practices."
- **What still failed:** It's still audience-agnostic. "Increase upload frequency" and "use Shorts" would be identical advice for a true-crime channel or a cooking channel. It doesn't know who watches worship music or why.
- **What I'd try next:** Tell it who my audience actually is.

## Version 2 — Add a defined audience

**Prompt:**
> Help me grow my worship music YouTube channel — specifically, help me figure out how to get from 200 to 1,000 subscribers over the next 3 months. My audience is Christians who use worship music for personal prayer, meditation, and background music during devotional or quiet time, mostly ages 25-55.

**Output (representative excerpt):**
> For a devotional/prayer-time audience, growth tactics look different than typical "hype" channels:
> - **Optimize for "functional" search terms** your audience actually types: "worship music for prayer," "soaking worship instrumental," "peaceful worship background music for sleep/study."
> - **Longer, loopable tracks** perform well here since people play them during extended quiet time rather than clicking through quickly.
> - **Community tab posts with a verse or short prayer** tend to build loyalty better than typical "like and subscribe" asks — your audience responds to spiritual value, not hype.
> - **Consistency around specific times of day** (early morning, evening) may matter since your audience has devotional routines.
> - Comment engagement can lean into prayer requests / testimonies rather than generic replies.

**Notes:**
- **What changed in the prompt:** Added a defined audience — who watches, why, and roughly what age range.
- **What actually improved in the output:** This is where it stopped sounding like a marketing textbook. Tactics were now specific to worship/devotional content — search terms my actual audience would type, the insight about loopable/longer tracks for quiet-time use, and reframing subscriber asks around spiritual value instead of generic YouTuber hype. It stopped explaining basics an audience-aware creator already knows (like "use SEO") and instead got into what SEO terms.
- **What still failed:** It's still theoretical — it doesn't know anything about my actual channel (how I make the music, what's worked so far, my current posting habits).
- **What I'd try next:** Give it real context about my channel instead of letting it guess.

## Version 3 — Add real context

**Prompt:**
> Help me grow my worship music YouTube channel — specifically, help me figure out how to get from 200 to 1,000 subscribers over the next 3 months. My audience is Christians who use worship music for personal prayer, meditation, and background music during devotional or quiet time, mostly ages 25-55.
>
> Context: I post one AI-generated instrumental worship track per week (made with Suno), videos are usually 5-10 minutes long, I'm at 200 subscribers after 6 months, and most of my views come from a handful of older uploads that happen to have SEO-friendly titles. I don't currently post Shorts or Community posts.

**Output (representative excerpt):**
> Given that a handful of older videos are already carrying most of your views, that's your strongest signal — here's how to build on it instead of guessing:
> - **Reverse-engineer your top performers.** Pull the titles/thumbnails of your best 3-5 videos and figure out the common thread (is it the mood word, a specific use-case like "sleep" or "study," or the title structure?). Apply that pattern going forward instead of varying titles randomly.
> - **Turn your best tracks into Shorts.** A 30-60 second loop of your top-performing track, captioned with the full title, is close to zero extra work and can funnel new viewers to the full track.
> - **Start Community posts** — even something simple like "which mood should next week's track be: peaceful piano or ambient strings?" builds engagement cheaply since you have zero right now.
> - **Consider a "best of" playlist** compiling your handful of proven winners — this gives new visitors an easy entry point instead of a random one-per-week upload history.

**Notes:**
- **What changed in the prompt:** Added concrete facts about the channel — cadence, tools used (Suno), video length, current performance pattern (a few old videos carrying most views), and what I haven't tried yet (Shorts, Community posts).
- **What actually improved in the output:** The advice stopped being aspirational and started being diagnostic. It picked up on the detail that a few old videos are outperforming everything else and built a whole strategy around exploiting that instead of suggesting I "post more" blindly. It also stopped suggesting things I'd already ruled out and instead pointed at the two specific gaps I mentioned (Shorts, Community).
- **What still failed:** The output is still just a wall of prose. If I actually want to act on this, I have to do the work of turning it into a prioritized to-do list myself.
- **What I'd try next:** Specify an output format so it's directly actionable.

Version 3's answer explained *why* to reverse-engineer top titles (look for the common thread — mood word vs. use-case vs. structure) — that nuance got compressed into "Matches known search intent," which is nearly meaningless on its own. The table also lost the connective logic between tactics (e.g., that the Shorts tactic depends on first identifying your top tracks). A rigid format traded depth for scannability, and for a genuinely diagnostic recommendation, that was a real loss, not a neutral one.
- **What I'd try next:** Add constraints so the list narrows to what I can *actually* do, and accept that I may need to ask for the "why" column to be a full sentence rather than a fragment.

## Version 5 — Add constraints

**Prompt:**
> Help me grow my worship music YouTube channel — specifically, help me figure out how to get from 200 to 1,000 subscribers over the next 3 months. My audience is Christians who use worship music for personal prayer, meditation, and background music during devotional or quiet time, mostly ages 25-55.
>
> Context: I post one AI-generated instrumental worship track per week (made with Suno), videos are usually 5-10 minutes long, I'm at 200 subscribers after 6 months, and most of my views come from a handful of older uploads that happen to have SEO-friendly titles. I don't currently post Shorts or Community posts.
>
> Constraints: I only have about 2 hours per week total to spend on marketing. I don't want to run paid ads. I don't want to hide that the music is AI-generated, but I also don't want tactics that make the channel look spammy or risk running into platform policies around AI-generated content disclosure.
>
> Format your answer as a short prioritized list (not a table) — for each item give the tactic, a one-sentence reason it works for this audience, and the weekly time cost.

**Output (representative excerpt):**
> Given your 2-hour weekly budget, here's what actually fits, in priority order:
> 1. **Reverse-engineer your top 3-5 videos' titles** (30 min, one-time then reused) — they're already proving what search terms and framing your audience responds to, so copying that pattern is higher-leverage than testing new ideas blindly.
> 2. **Clip one 30-45 second Short per week from your existing upload** (45 min) — this reuses content you're already making rather than creating new work, and Shorts are currently one of the easiest discovery funnels on the platform.
> 3. **Add a short, honest note in your video descriptions that tracks are AI-composed** (10 min, one-time template) — this keeps you compliant and builds trust with an audience that values integrity, rather than looking evasive about it later.
> 4. **One Community post per week, tied to a verse or theme** (15 min) — low effort, and this audience engages with reflective content rather than typical hype prompts.
>
> Cut for now: paid promotion (excluded), daily posting (not enough time), and a full site/social media presence (outside your budget).

**Notes:**
- **What changed in the prompt:** Added explicit constraints (2 hrs/week, no paid ads, must stay transparent about AI use without looking spammy) and reverted the format request to prose-with-structure instead of a rigid table.
- **What actually improved in the output:** The list got shorter and realistic — it explicitly cut tactics that didn't fit the time budget instead of leaving me to filter them myself, and it added a disclosure-friendly tactic (the AI-transparency note) that didn't appear in any earlier version because nothing before this asked for it. Reverting away from the strict table also brought back the one-sentence reasoning I'd lost in Version 4, without going back to the unstructured wall of text from Version 3.
- **What still failed:** It's making an assumption about "compliance" norms for AI-disclosure that I haven't actually verified are accurate for YouTube's current policy — I'd want to check that myself rather than trust it at face value.
- **What I'd try next:** Add a verification instruction telling it to flag any claim about platform policy as something I should confirm independently, rather than stating it as settled fact.

## Final Reusable Prompt

> Help me grow my [type of] YouTube channel — specifically, help me get from [current subscriber count] to [target] subscribers over the next [timeframe].
>
> **Audience:** [Who watches, why they watch, rough age range/context.]
>
> **Context:** [Upload cadence, tools/process used, video length, current performance pattern — what's working and what hasn't been tried yet.]
>
> **Constraints:** [Time budget per week, things you won't do (e.g. paid ads), and any non-negotiables like transparency or platform policy concerns.]
>
> **Format:** Give me a short prioritized list (not a rigid table). For each tactic include: the action, one sentence on *why* it works for this specific audience, and the weekly time cost. Explicitly tell me what you're cutting and why.
>
> **Verification:** If you state anything as a platform policy or "best practice" fact (e.g. algorithm behavior, disclosure requirements), flag it clearly as something I should double-check myself rather than presenting it as settled.

This works for a stranger on the same track because every bracket is a fill-in-the-blank, none of the language depends on knowing my channel already, and the format/verification instructions travel with the prompt regardless of topic.
